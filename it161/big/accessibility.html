Accessibility

Nowadays, one billion people, or 15% of the world’s population, experience some form of disability. Disable people are struggling to access and use some websites because these websites do not consider accessibility in their design.

Accessibility in web design aims to make websites accessible by everyone, especially people with disabilities (Shawn Lawton Henry). For example, some websites use specialized software called screen readers to make the blind able to perceive and navigate their content. Accessibility helps people experiencing some form of disability in auditory, cognitive, neurological, physical, speech, and visual.

Despite the common belief that accessibility is only useful for the disabled, accessibility usefulness goes beyond only benefiting the disabled people. It can benefits people without disabilities in certain situations such as changing ability due to aging, a broken arm, or lost glasses. Furthermore, improving accessibility on the website will expand potential audiences. Obviously the more people are able to access a website, the more impact the website can have (Ellice).

To make websites accessible to everybody, we can follow the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG 2.0) (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines). WCAG 2.0 provides useful recommendations for making content accessible. Some of these recommendations are as follows: Adding enough color contrast.

Not using color alone to make critical information understandable. Writing useful alternative text for your images and other non-text content. Ensuring sufficient contrast between text and its background. Supporting keyboard navigation. Enable Resizable Text That Doesn’t Break Your Site.

In addition to the aforementioned guidelines, there are many technologies helping the disabled to have better accessibility such as: Pseudo-Assistive Technology This technology provides useful functionalities such as text-to-speech functionality to help the disabled. Special Input Devices Such devices help people who cannot use common devices used to interact with computers such as traditional keyboards. Technology for Auditory Disabilities Such devices help people who have hearing disabilities to interact with computers.

As a designer, our mission is to empower people to get online. With web accessibility, we create a website to be used and enjoyed by all people regardless of their ability, age, or geographic location. (Web Accessibility Standards for People with Disabilities).
